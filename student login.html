Editor’s Picks

The Violet and the Tom

Eve Ocotillo




The Violet and the Tom

Page 1

My first taste of what it meant to be the slave of royalty came at his feet. The smooth pungency of a well-aged cheese placed carelessly between my lips.

Page 2

The fragrant sugar of a ripe summer peach, with the salt from the cheese—or was that simply his skin?—still lingering on his fingertips.

Page 3

It was solstice feast and I knelt beside him, my eyes trained only towards the floor or at his fingers, back straight, hands folded in my lap just so.

Page 4

Demure, and quietly alluring…or so I hoped. Because I truly did want him pleased—to believe me worth the price the King had paid.

Page 5

He seemed to have calmed since I'd been presented to him. At least, rage no longer vibrated from his frame.

Page 6

I was hopeful that he'd come to accept me—once he took the trouble to actually look at me. Eros, he was beautiful,

Page 7

and I was yet giddy with the thought, nibbling memories of the glances I'd stolen, wondering at my good fortune.

Page 8

Because although we exist only for our masters' pleasure, that existence is so much more pleasant when the man isn't a doddering, indolent gourmand.

Page 9

This man was still young, forty years, give or take, the lines around his eyes still faint, the merest touch of gray at his temples.

Page 10

He was darker than most men of the Isles and I wondered if exotic blood ran in his veins. Though several cents shorter than I, he was feline-sleek.

From the Earth to the Moon

Jules Verne




From the Earth to the Moon

Page 1

A spectator endowed with infinite power of sight, and placed at the unknown centre round which gravitates the universe, would have seen myriads of atoms

Page 2

filling all space during the chaotic epoch of creation. But by degrees, as centuries went on, a change took place; a law of gravitation manifested itself which the wandering atoms

Page 3

combined chemically according to their affinities, formed themselves into molecules, and made those nebulous masses with which the depths of the heavens are strewed.

Page 4

These masses were immediately animated by a movement of rotation round their central point. This centre, made of vague molecules, began to turn on itself whilst progressively

Page 5

condensing; then, following the immutable laws of mechanics, in proportion as its volume became diminished by condensation its movement of rotation

Page 6

was accelerated, and these two effects persisting, there resulted a principal planet, the centre of the nebulous mass. By watching attentively the

Page 7

spectator would then have seen other molecules in the mass behave like the central planet, and condense in the same manner by a movement of progressively-accelerated rotation,

Page 8

and gravitate round it under the form of innumerable stars. The nebulae, of which astronomers count nearly 5,000 at present, were formed.

Page 9

Amongst these 5,000 nebulae there is one that men have called the Milky Way, and which contains eighteen millions of stars, each of which has become the centre of a solar.

Page 10

If the spectator had then concentrated all his attention on these planets he would have seen them behave exactly like the sun and give birth to one or more cosmic rings.

A war that saved my life

Kimberly Brubaker




A war that saved my life

Page 1

“Ada! Get back from that window!” Mam’s voice, shouting. Mam’s arm, grabbing mine, yanking me so I toppled off my chair and fell hard to the floor.

Page 2

“I was only saying hello to Stephen White.” I knew better than to talk back, but sometimes my mouth was faster than my brain. I’d become a fighter, that summer.

Page 3

Mam smacked me. Hard. My head snapped back against the chair leg and for a moment I saw stars. “Don’t you be talkin’ to nobody!” Mam said. “I let you look out that window

Page 4

out a’ the kindness of my heart, but I’ll board it over if you go stickin’ your nose out, much less talkin’ to anyone!”.“Jamie’s out there,” I mumbled.

Page 5

“And why shouldn’t he be?” Mam said. “He ain’t a cripple. Not like you. ”I clamped my lips over what I might have said next, and shook my head to clear it.

Page 6

Then I saw the smear of blood on the floor. Oh, mercy. I hadn’t cleaned it all up from this afternoon. If Mam saw it, she’d put two and two together, fast.

Page 7

Then I’d be in the soup for sure. I slid over until my bottom covered the bloodstain, and I curled my bad foot beneath me.

Page 8

“You’d better be making my tea,” Mam said. She sat on the edge of the bed and peeled off her stockings, wiggling her two good feet near my face. “I’m off to work in a bit.”

Page 9

“Yes, Mam.” I pushed my window chair sideways to hide the blood. I crawled across the floor, keeping my scabbed-over bad foot out of Mam’s line of sight.

Page 10

I pulled myself onto our second chair, lit the gas ring, and put the kettle on. “Cut me some bread and dripping, Mam said.

The Time Machine

H. G. Wells




The Time Machine

Page 1

The Time Traveller (for so it will be convenient to speak of him) was expounding a recondite matter to us. His pale grey eyes shone and twinkled, and his usually pale

Page 2

face was flushed and animated. The fire burnt brightly, and the soft radiance of the incandescent lights in the lilies of silver caught the bubbles that flashed and passed in our glasses.

Page 3

Our chairs, being his patents, embraced and caressed us rather than submitted to be sat upon, and there was that luxurious after-dinner atmosphere,

Page 4

when thought runs gracefully free of the trammels of precision. And he put it to us in this way—marking the points with a lean forefinger—as we sat and lazily

Page 5

admired his earnestness over this new paradox (as we thought it) and his fecundity. “You must follow me carefully. I shall have to controvert

Page 6

one or two ideas that are almost universally accepted. The geometry, for instance, they taught you at school is founded on a misconception.”

Page 7

“Is not that rather a large thing to expect us to begin upon?” said Filby, an argumentative person with red hair. “I do not mean to ask you to accept anything without reasonable.

Page 8

You will soon admit as much as I need from you. You know of course that a mathematical line, a line of thickness nil, has no real existence. They taught you that?

Page 9

Filby became pensive. “Clearly,” the Time Traveller proceeded, “any real body must have extension in four directions: it must have Length, Breadth, Thickness, and—Duration.

Page 10

“Now, it is very remarkable that this is so extensively overlooked,” continued the Time Traveller, with a slight accession of cheerfulness.

The Tiles Of The Core

Michael E. Thies




The Tiles Of The Core

Page 1

Even past midday, Freyr didn’t have much bearing on the stone court. Lord’s Keep was too tall, and the red sun was not even at its zenith yet.

Page 2

In its current position, the two sparring figures produced slender shadows dancing in rhythmic beats of one another. Hydro Paen’s younger brother, Aiton,

Page 3

looked on closely from the shaded portion of the castle walls. Aiton sat in a long chair, held tight by his mother. His mother’s friends were there too—marchionesses from

Page 4

different provinces of Acquava. Elias Ward, his father’s adored, had also taken a spot amongst the crowd, waiting to tend to any injury. Hydro tried not to focus on them,

Page 5

but every so often he caught himself stealing a gaze, watching for his mother’s eye, which never seemed to look. Mostly Hydro focused on the man in front of him: Korth.

Page 6

His trainer swung his sword upward, forcing Hydro into a backflip. Upon landing, he ducked to avoid another stroke. Using his momentum, Hydro propelled his body forward,

Page 7

lance in front, attempting to pierce Korth’s seachrome armor. He missed. Back exposed, he tugged on the golden chain attached to his sapphire lance and spun around.

Page 8

“Good reflexes, Prince,” said Korth. His thick moustache bobbed up and down beneath the gap of his seachrome helm. “I do not need your praise, Korth.”

Page 9

Hydro jabbed his lance forward, but it was batted away. A heavy boot struck his stomach, causing his chest to heave inward. Defeat rang thrice as the lance clattered on the court.

Page 10

“You may not need my praise, but you certainly need my help. Your lance skills are still mediocre.” Korth grinned, extending his sea-leather glove, dotted in brine, downed.
